The Problem

Why Every AI Post Sounds Like Corporate Slop

Most people open ChatGPT, type "write me a LinkedIn post about leadership," and wonder why it sounds like a press release from 2012.

That's not prompting. That's praying.

The real problem: you're asking AI to write content without telling it WHO you are. Claude has a default voice. ChatGPT has a default voice. Gemini has a default voice. And none of them are yours. You're publishing their voice, not yours.

After hundreds of prompts and thousands of outputs testing this across ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, and Claude, we cracked the problem. The fix is a Voice DNA extraction process that takes about 10 minutes and permanently changes the way AI writes for you.

Step 1: The Framework

The Voice DNA Extraction Framework

Your voice has five distinct layers. Most AI tools only see the surface. This framework maps all five, so Claude can replicate the full picture.

01

Voice Attributes

How you sound at the personality level. Your humor patterns, how you treat the reader, how you signal authority, whether you write like you're in conversation or delivering a lecture. These are the traits that make someone read a post and immediately know it's you.

02

Vocabulary Profile

The words you reach for vs. the words you'd never say. This produces three lists: your Power Words (words you use constantly that are distinctly yours), your Avoid Words (AI-tell words like "game-changer," "leverage," "delve" that erode your voice), and Replacement pairs (what you'd actually say instead).

03

Sentence Fingerprint

The rhythm and structure of your sentences. Average length, how often you punch short, whether you start sentences with "But" or "And," how long your paragraphs run. This is your writing's musical fingerprint. It's why some posts feel like you and some don't, even when the words seem right.

04

Quirk Quotient

The small signature moves that make your writing unmistakably yours. Maybe you always ask a question at the end. Maybe you use one-word sentences for emphasis. Maybe you open with a story. Quirks are what make someone read your content and go "yep, that's definitely them."

05

Tone Grid Position

Where you sit on two axes: formal vs. casual, and tight vs. loose. Are you Apple-polished or Basecamp-conversational? This calibrates the exact register Claude writes in, so it never swings too corporate or too sloppy for your brand.

Step 2: The Prompts

5 Copy-Paste Prompts That Train Claude on Your Voice

Use these in order. Each one builds on the last. By Prompt 5, Claude is writing in your voice, not its default. Paste them directly into Claude.

Prompt 1 — Voice DNA Extraction
I'm going to paste 3-5 samples of my real writing. Read them carefully and extract my Voice DNA. First, identify my top 10 voice attributes — the personality traits evident in how I write. Look for: how I relate to the reader, whether I use humor and how, my authority style, my use of first vs second person, how conversational vs formal I am. Then identify my top style attributes: my word choice sophistication, sentence structure patterns, rhythm and pacing, point of view consistency, and what domains I draw figurative language from (not specific metaphors — just domains, like "tech" or "sports" or "cooking"). Present your findings with direct quotes from my writing as evidence. Don't just list traits — show me the proof. Here are my writing samples: [PASTE 3-5 SAMPLES OF YOUR REAL WRITING — LINKEDIN POSTS, EMAILS, NEWSLETTERS, ANYTHING YOU WROTE]
Prompt 2 — Vocabulary Audit
Based on the writing samples I shared, build my Vocabulary Profile with three lists: 1. Power Words (10-15 words): The words I reach for repeatedly that are distinctly mine. These must come from my actual writing — don't suggest words I've never used. 2. Avoid Words (10-15 words): Words to cut from AI output. Include any AI-tell words you spotted in my samples ("game-changer," "leverage," "crucial," "delve," "supercharge," "landscape," "unlock," "elevate"). Be honest if I'm using them — that's useful information. 3. Replacement Pairs (5-10): What I'd actually say instead of the generic version. Format as: [generic word] → [my version]. Format the whole thing as something I can paste directly into any AI prompt as instructions.
Prompt 3 — Sentence Fingerprint
Analyze my sentence patterns across five dimensions. Use actual counts from my samples, not estimates. 1. Length — average words per sentence, my preferred range, and how I use short punchy sentences for emphasis. 2. Structure — what percentage are simple vs compound vs complex sentences? 3. Beginnings — how do my sentences start? Do I use "But" or "And" openers? Questions? Subject-first? Give me the actual patterns with percentages if you can. 4. Transitions — which connector words do I use? Which do I conspicuously avoid? 5. Rhythm — how long are my paragraphs on average? How often do I ask questions? Do I use commands? Build this into a Sentence Style Guide I can drop into any AI prompt as mechanical instructions. Make it specific enough that Claude can follow it exactly.
Prompt 4 — Voice Template Compiler
Compile everything we've built into a single Voice Prompt Template I can paste into any AI tool — Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini — and immediately get writing that sounds like me. Structure it exactly like this: Write in the style of [MY NAME]. Voice Attributes: - [from our voice DNA extraction] Tone: - [position on formal/casual and tight/loose axes] - Humor style: [what we found] Style: - [from voice DNA style analysis] - Power Words: [list] - Avoid Words: [list] - Replacements: [pairs] Sentence Structure: - [from sentence fingerprint] - Paragraph length: [finding] - Signature moves: [distinctive patterns] Additional Instructions: - [anything else that makes my writing mine] This template must work completely standalone. Someone should be able to paste it into any AI with zero extra context and get output that sounds like me.
Prompt 5 — Test and Calibrate
[PASTE YOUR FULL VOICE TEMPLATE HERE] Now use this Voice Template to write a LinkedIn post about: [YOUR TOPIC] After you write it, score it 1-10 on how much it sounds like me. Then flag everything that feels off — wrong word choice, wrong rhythm, too formal, too casual, anything that breaks the voice. For each flagged section, rewrite it in my actual voice and explain exactly what changed and why. Show me: - Original output - Flagged sections (with reason) - Revised version - What you learned to remember for next time
Step 3: Avoid These

The 3 Fatal Mistakes That Make Every AI Post Sound Robotic

Mistake 01 Prompting for content instead of voice

You open Claude and type "write a LinkedIn post about how I scaled my team from 5 to 50 people." Claude writes a post. It sounds professional. It also sounds like it could have been written by literally anyone in your industry.

The problem: you gave Claude a topic. You never gave it you. Without your Voice Template leading the prompt, Claude defaults to its own voice, not yours.

The Fix Always lead with your Voice Template before the topic. The structure is: [full voice template] + [topic + any context]. Your voice instructions come first, every single time. Topic second. This order is non-negotiable.
Mistake 02 Skipping the Vocabulary Audit

AI tools have a default vocabulary. Left to their own devices, they reach for "crucial," "leverage," "game-changing," "foster," "supercharge," and "delve." These words are AI fingerprints. Every reader who has seen AI content before knows exactly what they are when they appear in your post.

This one issue is responsible for more AI-detection failures than anything else. Not the ideas. Not the structure. The vocabulary.

The Fix Build your Avoid Words list using Prompt 2 above and paste it in every single prompt you send. Your Power Words list works the same way. This is the fastest single change you can make to immediately close the gap between AI output and real human writing.
Mistake 03 Treating the first output as final

Voice cloning is not a one-shot process. The first time you use your Voice Template, the output will be roughly 70% you. That's already dramatically better than zero context. But getting to 95% requires iteration, and most people stop at that first draft.

Every output you don't evaluate and feed back is a missed calibration opportunity.

The Fix Use Prompt 5 every time, for at least the first 10 posts. Score the output. Flag what's off. Get the rewrite. Then update your Voice Template based on the pattern of corrections. By post 10, Claude is catching things you didn't even explicitly tell it to catch.

Advanced: The Context Graph

The Method That Makes Claude Better Over Time

Your Voice Template is a living document. The Context Graph method is what turns it from a static prompt into something that compounds. Here's how it works:

01

Generate and Label

Every time Claude writes something for you, label the output. "Hit" if it sounds like you. "Miss" if something's off. Add a one-sentence note on why it missed.

02

Spot the Pattern

After 5-10 outputs, look at your Miss labels. Is Claude consistently too formal? Too corporate in word choice? Too long in paragraphs? The pattern is usually one or two specific things, not everything at once.

03

Update the Template

Add the pattern you found as a specific instruction in your Voice Template. "Never use passive voice" or "always end with a question" or "paragraphs max 2 sentences." Specific, mechanical instructions outperform abstract ones.

04

Add Hit Examples

When a post is a strong Hit, paste the full text into your template under an "Example Output" section. After 3 strong examples, Claude has a concrete target, not just abstract instructions.

05

Repeat Until Indistinguishable

By round 10-15, you'll have a Voice Template dense enough that Claude starts catching voice issues you hadn't explicitly told it to catch. That's the inflection point. One of our clients said: "This sounds more like me than what I actually write." That's where you're aiming.
